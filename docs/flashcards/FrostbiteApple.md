## Tree Traits

### Canadian Hardiness Zone
- Zone 3 (hardy to -35°F / Zone 3b)

### Soil Preference
- Average to moist, well-drained soil. Not particularly fussy about soil type 
or pH. Keep mulched and avoid allowing to dry out completely.

### Flowering Time
- Late — last week of May to first week of June

### Bloom Colour
- White with shell-pink overtones, opening from distinctive pink buds

### Pollination Requirements
- Not self-fertile. Requires a different apple or crabapple variety blooming 
at a similar time nearby. Compatible pollinators include Cortland, Honeycrisp, 
Fuji, and Dolgo Crabapple. Any crabapple within a quarter mile will likely 
suffice due to their long bloom period.

### Sun/Shade Requirements
- Full sun (6–8 hours daily)

### General Growth Habit
- Low to medium vigour, spreading form. Grows to approximately 20 ft tall and 
wide on standard rootstock. Produces fruit on spurs. High maintenance — 
requires regular care and upkeep.

### Overall Disease Resistance Rating
- Moderately scab resistant. Prone to russeting (cracking near the calyx), 
which can attract wasps and increase susceptibility to mold near harvest.

---

## Fruit Traits

### Recommended Use
- Fresh eating, cider-making

### Fruit Size
- Small — approximately 2 to 2.5 inches in diameter. Striped maroon-red over 
golden yellow skin. Flesh is cream to light yellow, firm and very juicy.

### Storage
- Relatively short storage life due to russeting. Best used soon after harvest. 
Refrigerate and consume within a few weeks.

### Harvest
- Late September to early October

## Fun Facts
- Frostbite is the parent of Keepsake and Sweet Sixteen, and the grandparent 
of the famous Honeycrisp apple.
- Tasters describe the flavour as intensely sweet with notes of cane sugar, 
molasses, and tropical fruit (pineapple, lychee).
- Survived the killing Minnesota winter of 1917–18.

### Illustrations and/or Photos
- https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flowers_of_%27Minnesota_447%27_apple.jpg

### History
- Originated as an open-pollinated seedling of Melinda at the University of 
Minnesota in 1921. Previously known as MN 447. Despite being developed in the 
1920s, it was not officially released until 2008 at the request of orchardists. 
Names considered before release included "Munchkin" and "Small Wonder."

### Cultural Uses
- Valued as a fresh eating and cider apple in cold northern climates where few 
quality apples can grow. Has been a cornerstone of the University of 
Minnesota's cold-hardy apple breeding program for over a century.
