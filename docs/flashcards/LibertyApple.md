## Tree Traits

### Canadian Hardiness Zone
- Zone 4

### Soil Preference
- Sandy loam, loam, or clay loam. Prefers average to moist, well-drained 
conditions. Avoid planting anywhere that floods for more than two weeks 
in the spring.

### Flowering Time
- Early to mid-season (April–May). Early-flowering — avoid low frost 
pockets.

### Bloom Colour
- White to pink

### Pollination Requirements
- Requires a pollinator of a different apple variety blooming at the 
same time. Good pollinators include McIntosh, Freedom, and Golden 
Delicious. Also acts as a good pollinator for others.

### Sun/Shade Requirements
- Full sun (approx. 8–10 hours of sun daily)

### General Growth Habit
- Moderately vigorous, spreading, spur-bearing. Precocious and a 
reliable heavy cropper. Bears fruit 3–4 years after planting.

### Overall Disease Resistance Rating
- Excellent. Very resistant to apple scab and cedar-apple rust. 
Resistant to fireblight, powdery mildew, and quince rust. Not resistant 
to apple insect pests — standard insect management still required.

## Fruit Traits

### Recommended Use
- Fresh eating, baking, cider, sauce

### Fruit Size
- Medium to large. Bright red over yellow skin. Crisp, juicy, ivory 
to pale yellow flesh. Sweet-tart with a vinous, cider-like aroma.

### Storage
- 1–2 months. Flavor develops and improves over the first month in 
cold storage. Harvest window is narrow — pick promptly when ripe.

### Harvest
- Late September to early October

## Fun Facts
- Named "Liberty" to denote freedom from disease — one of the most 
disease-resistant apple varieties ever developed.
- Its disease resistance comes from the *Vf* gene inherited from 
*Malus floribunda*, a Japanese flowering crabapple.
- The first variety released from the Cornell/NYSAES partnership.

### History
- Developed at the New York State Agricultural Experiment Station in 
Geneva, NY. Seedling produced in 1955 from crossing Macoun with Purdue 
54-12 (a line carrying *Malus floribunda* disease resistance). Released 
to the public in 1978 by Dr. Robert Lamb.

### Cultural Uses
- Widely grown as a low-spray and organic orchard variety. Popular 
replacement for McIntosh in home orchards due to its disease resistance 
and similar flavour profile.
