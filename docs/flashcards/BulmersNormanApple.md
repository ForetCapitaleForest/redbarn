## Tree Traits

### Canadian Hardiness Zone
- Zone 4

### Soil Preference
- Sandy loam, loam, or clay loam. Prefers average to moist, well-drained
conditions. Avoid planting anywhere that floods for more than two weeks
in the spring.

### Flowering Time
- Middle

### Bloom Colour
- White

### Pollination Requirements
- Triploid — sterile and cannot pollinate other trees. Requires a
pollinator of a different apple variety blooming at the same time in
order to produce fruit.

### Sun/Shade Requirements
- Full sun (approx. 8–10 hours of sun daily)

### General Growth Habit
- Vigorous, spreading. Tends toward biennial bearing with heavy crops.
Branches brittle under heavy crop load. Also historically used as
rootstock for grafting other varieties.

### Overall Disease Resistance Rating
- High. Resistant to fireblight, mildew, and brown rot. Susceptible
to apple scab.

## Fruit Traits

### Recommended Use
- Cider only. Bittersweet classification — adds tannin and body to
blends. Not suited for fresh eating.

### Fruit Size
- Medium. Conical. Greenish-yellow skin. Produces a sweet, astringent
juice that ferments rapidly.

### Storage
- A couple of weeks in cool, humid conditions.

### Harvest
- September — Late

### Cider Class
- Class: Bittersweet
- Sugar: Medium, SG 1.056
- Acidity: Low, TA 3.4 g/L
- Tannins: Medium
- Juice Yield: Medium to high
- Taste: Mildly bitter with an astringent aftertaste
- Recommendations: Helps to reduce acidity and increase tannins in cider blends
  
  *Information based on Claude Jolicoeur's The New Cidermaker's Handbook and Washington State University's "Cultivar Performance Gallery"

## Fun Facts
- When first brought to England it had no name — simply an unnamed
variety from Normandy that quickly proved its worth as a cider apple.
- Now used primarily as rootstock rather than for its fruit, with more
prized cider varieties grafted onto its sturdy trunk.
- Produces hard, bitter tannins that add significant body and texture
when blended with sharper cider varieties.

### History
- Found growing in Normandy, France and imported to England in the
early 1890s by H.P. Bulmer & Co. of Hereford. Widely planted across
Herefordshire during the early 1900s as a cider staple. Has since
fallen out of commercial favour but remains valued by serious cider
makers and as a grafting rootstock.

### Cultural Uses
- Traditional English bittersweet cider apple. Used as a blending
variety to add tannin and body. Historically important as a grafting
rootstock.
