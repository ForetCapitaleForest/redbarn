## Tree Traits

### Canadian Hardiness Zone
- Zone 3

### Soil Preference
- Sandy loam, loam, or clay loam. Prefers average to moist, well-drained
conditions. Avoid planting anywhere that floods for more than two weeks
in the spring.

### Flowering Time
- Middle

### Bloom Colour
- White

### Pollination Requirements
- Requires a pollinator of a different apple variety blooming at the
same time.

### Sun/Shade Requirements
- Full sun (approx. 8–10 hours of sun daily)

### General Growth Habit
- Vigorous, spur-bearing. Heavy crops but tends toward biennial bearing.
Has a notable tendency to produce fused fruits (multiple apples growing
together on one stem). Growth is sprawling and requires detailed,
thoughtful pruning.

### Overall Disease Resistance Rating
- High. Resistant to scab and brown rot. Somewhat susceptible to
fireblight under heavy pressure, though vigorous habit tends to outgrow
minor infections.

## Fruit Traits

### Recommended Use
- Cider only.

### Fruit Size
- Medium. Often conjoined/fused fruits. Dark red skin, creamy flesh.
Highly acidic juice with petrol, mineral, and bright fruit notes.

### Storage
- Does not store well. Fruit should be pressed within two weeks of
harvest.

### Harvest
- October — Mid

### Cider Class
- Class: Bittersharp
- Sugar: High, SG 1.060
- Acidity: Very high, TA 15 g/L
- Tannins: Medium, 3 g/L
- Juice Yield: Medium, 253 m/L
- Taste: Low astringency
- Recommendations: Can be made into a single-variety cider but it is best in blends
  
  *Information based on Claude Jolicoeur's The New Cidermaker's Handbook and Washington State University's "Cultivar Performance Gallery"

## Fun Facts
- Identifiable by its fused fruits — multiple apples sometimes growing
together on a single stem with multiple cores.
- Has shown promising results at the Vineland Research Station in
Ontario — performing well in the Canadian climate unlike many other
European cider varieties.
- Extremely popular in England at the turn of the 20th century before
its commercial decline after the 1910s.

### Illustrations and/or Photos

### History
- Originated in Somerset, England, some time in the 1800s. First grown
in the orchard of Charles Porter in East Lambrook, England, pre-1900.
Became extremely popular across England before waning after the 1910s.
Still grown today and showing renewed promise in North American cider
orchards.

### Cultural Uses
- Traditional English bittersharp cider apple from Somerset. Used both
as a single-variety cider and in blends to add high acid and body.
